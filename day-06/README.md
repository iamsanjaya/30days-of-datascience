## Key Concepts

### MCAR — Missing Completely At Random

No relationship between missingness and any data.
Max absolute correlation across all predictors ≈ 0.
Safe to drop rows or use simple (mean/median) imputation.

### MAR — Missing At Random

Missingness depends on other _observed_ columns, not the missing value itself.
At least one predictor correlates with the missing flag.
Impute using those correlated columns.

### MNAR — Missing Not At Random

Missingness depends on the missing value itself.
Cannot be detected statistically — requires domain knowledge.
Example: cash payments may not generate digital tip records;
tip_rate missingness may be caused by the payment type being cash.

## Notable Findings

- Green vs Yellow serve different geographies. Green has 68K trips, Yellow has 3M — a 98:2 split after merging. Any downstream model trained on this combined dataset will be dominated by Yellow taxi behaviour.
- The missingness cluster (passenger_count, RatecodeID, congestion_surcharge, store_and_fwd_flag) is identical at 6.3% in green — same rows, same fields. This is a vendor-level reporting failure, not random dropout. Classification: MAR on VendorID.
- payment_type is a MNAR candidate: cash payments may not log a record at all, meaning the missingness is caused by the value itself.
- tip_rate and speed_mph are structural NaNs created by the feature engineering formulas. They are not data quality issues.
